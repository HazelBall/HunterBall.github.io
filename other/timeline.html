The start of agriculture and farming, the domestication of animals, and many more important evolutions in humanity happened in this era. Although not many specific events happened during the 8th Millennium BC, many advancements in society and humanity occured. At the start of the millennium, almost all people on Earth were hunter-gatherers, with few Neolithic settlements in more densly packed areas. However, by the end of it, there were flourishing settlements with domesticated cats, cattle, pigs, and cultivated wheat, barley, and more. This millennium will not go down as the most important, but it led to civilization as we know it.

The Beginnings of Settlements

×

With a world population of over 5,000,000 Settlements in present day Turkey, Norway, England, Nigeria, and Denmark are established around this time. A settlement in Franchthi Cave in Peloponnese, continues. This settlement was continuously occupied since 20,000 BC and will last for nearly 5000 more years. Although there are no settlements, native hunter-gatherers populated most of the North American Great Plains at this time.

In northern Mesopotamia, now northern Iraq, cultivation of barley and wheat begins. At first they are used for beer, gruel, and soup, eventually for bread. In early agriculture at this time, the planting stick is used, but it is replaced by a primitive plow in subsequent centuries. Around this time, a round stone tower, now preserved to about 8.5 meters high and 8.5 meters in diameter is built in Jericho.

Bladed tools found in southwest Iran date from around 8000 BC; they were made from obsidian that had been transported from Anatolia.

Jericho Settlement Ends

×

End of the large settlement at Jericho. Jericho had the first known settlement-wide wall, and there is evidence there was a population of 2000-3000 and domesticated wheat and barley.
